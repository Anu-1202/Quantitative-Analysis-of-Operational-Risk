# Quantitative-Analysis-of-Operational-Risk
This project provides a comprehensive quantitative analysis of Operational Risk Class X, using ten years of historical loss data (964 events). The primary objective was to estimate the 99.9% Value at Risk (VaR) for regulatory capital purposes, navigating the challenges of extreme right-skewness and heavy-tailed distributions characteristic of "low-frequency, high-impact" operational events.Methodology: Extreme Value Theory (EVT)The initial analysis revealed a mean 3.5x larger than the median, immediately signaling a severe right-skew. Traditional distributions (Lognormal, Weibull, Gamma) were rejected by KS and AD tests, confirming that no single parametric model could capture both routine losses and catastrophic outliers.To solve this, I implemented a Two-Component Composite Model:The Body (65% of mass): Modeled non-parametrically using the Empirical CDF for losses below the €142,722 threshold.The Tail (35% of mass): Modeled using the Peaks Over Threshold (POT) methodology. A Generalized Pareto Distribution (GPD) was fitted to the exceedances.Key Technical FindingsThreshold Optimization: The 65th percentile was identified as the optimal threshold, yielding a near-perfect KS p-value of 0.9993.The Shape Parameter ($\xi$): The GPD fit produced a $\xi = 0.8456$. This is the most critical result, placing the risk class in the Fréchet domain of attraction. A $\xi > 0.5$ indicates a distribution with infinite variance, meaning extreme losses are theoretically unbounded and standard variance-based metrics are insufficient.Frequency Modeling: Annual event frequency was modeled via a Poisson distribution ($\lambda = 96.4$).Capital Estimation & ResultsThe 99.9% VaR was calculated using two distinct methods to ensure robustness:Monte Carlo Simulation (200,000 iterations): €814.16MSingle Loss Approximation (SLA): €789.52M (€820.31M Adjusted)The remarkable convergence (within 1%) between Monte Carlo and the Adjusted SLA validates the "Single-Loss Property" of this portfolio: because the tail is so heavy, the annual aggregate loss is dominated by one catastrophic event rather than the accumulation of many small ones. This project underscores the necessity of Extreme Value Theory in modern financial risk management.

Overall Conclusion for Your Report
"The analysis demonstrates that the operational losses of risk class X exhibit a severely heavy-tailed distribution that cannot be adequately captured by standard parametric distributions such as the Lognormal, Weibull, or Gamma. The Peaks Over Threshold approach, combining an empirical body with a GPD tail fitted above the 65th percentile threshold, provides an excellent fit to the data (KS p-value = 0.9993 on the tail). The estimated GPD shape parameter ξ = 0.8456 places the distribution firmly in the heavy-tailed Fréchet domain, with infinite variance and extreme sensitivity to tail events. The resulting 99.9% VaR of approximately €814M, consistently estimated by both Monte Carlo simulation and the Single Loss Approximation, reflects the catastrophic potential of this risk class and highlights the necessity of Extreme Value Theory methods for its proper quantification."
